**OPTION A - The contrast story (recommended)**

---

Garry Tan published gstack last week. 42,000 stars.

It turns one developer into a team that ships fast.

I built something similar, in silence, for a different problem.

AnchorStack turns one founder into a team that ships *right* - where being wrong has consequences.

---

I am building Umarise: infrastructure that proves specific bytes existed at a specific time, anchored in Bitcoin, independently verifiable, forever.

The claims this product makes carry legal weight.
A wrong sentence in investor materials destroys credibility.
An architectural shortcut that creates user dependency betrays the product's promise.
A legal formulation that does not survive scrutiny could end a deal.

I needed a way to hold all of that. Simultaneously. Without forgetting.

---

The insight: **a skill is not a prompt. A skill is an anchor.**

A prompt asks for output.
An anchor constrains it.

The difference is a mandatory checklist, validated formulations cleared for external use, hard constraints that accumulate and never expire, and one constitutional question that overrides everything else.

For Umarise, that question is:
*Does this choice make the user dependent on Umarise for the validity of their proof?*

One question. It has shaped more architecture decisions than any feature request.

---

I built 13 domain skills and one guardian over 6 months.
Solo. No team. No one reviewing my work except the process I built to review it.

Every overclaim that did not ship. Every architectural dependency that was caught before it was built. Every formulation that survived lawyer review and got locked for reuse.

All of it anchored in the process, not in my head.

---

Today I am publishing it.

Not as a product. As a methodology.

AnchorStack. MIT license. GitHub link in comments.

If you build something where the cost of being wrong is high - this is for you.
